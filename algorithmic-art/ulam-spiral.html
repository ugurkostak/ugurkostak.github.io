The Ulam Spiral is a simple but striking pattern discovered by mathematician Stanislaw Ulam in 1963. When natural numbers are arranged in a spiral and primes are highlighted, surprising diagonal and linear patterns emerge—suggesting deep mathematical structure underlying the distribution of primes.

This visualization displays the first 10,000 numbers arranged in a spiral pattern, with prime numbers highlighted in red, composites in dark gray, and the number 1 in green. Hover any cell to inspect its value and grid position.

Ulam reportedly drew the first version of this spiral while doodling during a dull conference talk. The diagonal stripes that appear correspond to prime-rich quadratic polynomials—a connection that, despite decades of work, mathematicians still cannot fully explain. Visualizations like this remind us that even the most studied objects in mathematics still hold open questions in plain sight.
